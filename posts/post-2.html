Beyond the Quota: Why Korea's Disability Employment Reform Needs Vocational Rehabilitation at Its Core

Jeongwon (Wonny) Kim  ·  April 12, 2026  ·  9 min read

This past February, Korea made a quiet but significant announcement: for the first time in eight years, the country would raise its mandatory disability employment quota for private sector companies. The rate climbs from 3.1% to 3.3% this year, with a phased trajectory reaching 3.9% by 2029. The public sector ceiling will follow, rising toward 4%. Alongside the quota increase, the Lee administration pledged a record 1 trillion won budget for disability employment programs through the Ministry of Employment and Labor.

On paper, this is progress. An eight-year freeze had left Korea's legal framework significantly behind its stated goals, and advocates had long called for action. But as someone who has worked within both the Korean and U.S. vocational rehabilitation systems, I find myself holding this news with a mixture of hope and caution. Because the numbers tell a more complicated story — and the story points directly to what comprehensive vocational rehabilitation is for.

The Paradox in the Data

While Korea's mandatory employment quota system has been in place for decades, the employment rate for people with disabilities has actually been declining. It stood at 50.3% in 2022, dropped to 49.8% in 2023, and fell again to 48.4% in 2024. Meanwhile, national employment rates have trended upward for the general population. Perhaps more striking: a separate 2024 survey found that fewer than 3 in 10 South Koreans with physical or mental disabilities were employed — a figure of just 28.9%.

This divergence matters. It tells us that compliance thresholds and employment rates are not the same thing, and that a higher quota number does not automatically translate into more meaningful participation in the labor market. In Seoul, 19 of 23 affiliated public agencies achieved the disability employment threshold by end of 2025 — an encouraging compliance rate of 83%. Yet penalty payments for non-compliance, while declining, still exist: 255 million won paid in 2025 alone. These penalties, it should be noted, can function as a fee that companies pay to opt out of hiring — trading obligation for cost rather than changing practice.

"A quota gets someone in the door. Vocational rehabilitation is what makes it possible for them to stay — and to grow."

This is the central tension. Quota policies are a lever of structural change, and I genuinely support their role. They shift employer behavior, signal social norms, and open doors that market forces alone would keep closed. But they cannot, on their own, address the skills gap, the accommodation gap, the attitude gap, or the wellbeing gap that many workers with disabilities face once they are hired. For that, you need the kind of individualized, evidence-based support that vocational rehabilitation was designed to provide.

What Quotas Can't Do

Let me be specific about where mandatory employment quotas reach their limits, because understanding this is essential for practitioners and policymakers alike.

Person-Level Gaps
  • Underdeveloped vocational skills or work history
  • Mental health barriers (anxiety, depression, PTSD)
  • Low self-efficacy and unclear vocational identity
  • Lack of job-seeking strategies and workplace literacy
  • Medical or functional limitations not yet accommodated
System-Level Gaps
  • Jobs offered without meaningful role design
  • Absent or tokenistic workplace accommodations
  • Supervisors untrained in disability inclusion
  • No ongoing job retention support after placement
  • Penalty-based compliance culture, not inclusion culture

Both columns matter. And addressing them requires professionals who are trained to work systematically at the intersection of person and environment — which is exactly the scope of vocational rehabilitation counseling and vocational evaluation.

The Evidence We Have: What Actually Works

If we want disability employment outcomes to genuinely improve alongside Korea's expanding quota ambitions, we should look carefully at the evidence base that has accumulated over the past three decades.

The most rigorously studied model is Individual Placement and Support (IPS), developed in the United States as a supported employment approach for people with serious mental illness. IPS operates on a deceptively simple principle: place first, train second. Rather than preparing people in sheltered settings before attempting competitive employment, IPS integrates employment specialists directly into clinical or rehabilitation teams, helps clients find real jobs in the community that match their preferences, and provides individualized support on the job for as long as needed.

The evidence for IPS is remarkable in a field where outcomes often disappoint. Systematic reviews show consistent, substantial advantages over conventional vocational services on job entry rates, time to employment, earnings, and work hours. A 2026 longitudinal study from Italy following people with severe mental illness found sustained positive employment outcomes over time, reinforcing what U.S. research has demonstrated for years. IPS has now been implemented in over 80% of U.S. states and in approximately 20 countries worldwide.

In Korea, the story of supported employment is one of unrealized potential. Academic analyses have noted that despite three decades since supported employment was introduced in Korea, the system has largely functioned as a training arrangement rather than a true employment model. Research has called for development of a "Korean-style supported employment model" — one that adapts the evidence base to Korea's labor market structures, family dynamics, and disability service ecosystem. This is, in my view, one of the most important development priorities for Korean vocational rehabilitation right now.

The Role of Vocational Evaluation

Before placement comes assessment. One of the things that drew me to pursue the Certified Vocational Evaluator (CVE) credential is my belief that good evaluation is the foundation of everything that follows. A thorough vocational evaluation does not simply document what a person cannot do — it maps the full landscape of their work-related strengths, interests, functional capacities, learning styles, and transferable skills. It provides the individualized data that makes person-centered planning possible.

What a Vocational Evaluation Addresses

Work samples and situational assessments — direct observation of how someone performs actual or simulated work tasks, capturing strengths that paper assessments miss.

Psychological and aptitude testing — standardized tools that identify cognitive patterns, learning needs, and vocational interests.

Functional capacity and accommodation needs — what physical, sensory, or cognitive adaptations would allow this person to perform optimally?

Labor market alignment — which occupational areas have realistic demand that intersects with this person's profile and location?

In the U.S. system, vocational evaluation findings feed directly into the Individualized Plan for Employment (IPE) — the roadmap for a person's rehabilitation journey. In Korea, the equivalent planning process exists within vocational rehabilitation centers (직업재활센터) and employment support services through the Korea Employment Agency for Persons with Disabilities (KEAD, 한국장애인고용공단). But the depth of evaluation practice, the training of evaluators, and the integration of evaluation findings into individualized planning varies widely.

Expanding Korea's mandatory quota without investing in the evaluation and counseling infrastructure to support job seekers with disabilities is like raising a doorway without building the path that leads to it. The door matters. So does the path.

Two Systems, One Goal

I came to vocational rehabilitation through the U.S. system, trained as a CRC with an understanding of federal-state VR programs, WIOA frameworks, and a strong tradition of person-centered, consumer-driven practice. Living and working now in Gangneung, I am learning — continually and sometimes humbly — that systems shape not just services but assumptions about disability, work, and what people deserve.

In the U.S., the Rehabilitation Act enshrines a presumption of employability: every person with a disability is presumed capable of competitive integrated employment unless a thorough assessment indicates otherwise. This is a radical philosophical premise when taken seriously. It shifts the burden of proof. It centers the person's goals, not the system's convenience.

Korea's approach has historically been more paternalistic — disability categories determine service eligibility in ways that can limit rather than expand options, and job-matching has sometimes been driven more by employer preferences than client goals. But I see movement. I see practitioners and researchers pushing for better models. I see the KEAD investing in vocational training infrastructure. And I see, in this year's quota reform, a government at least acknowledging that the status quo is not working.

"What matters in the end is not that a quota was met. What matters is whether a person found work that fits — work that uses their abilities, pays a living wage, and feels worth showing up for."

Both systems have things to learn from each other. Korea's deep investment in family and community networks, its emphasis on collective wellbeing, and its emerging interest in culturally adapted service models offer real strengths. The U.S. tradition of individualized planning, consumer self-determination, and evidence-based supported employment offers frameworks worth adapting. The best outcomes come from holding both.

What I'm Watching — and Hoping For

As Korea implements this quota increase over the next few years, here is what I believe will determine whether the employment lives of people with disabilities actually improve:

Investment in vocational evaluation capacity. Training more qualified vocational evaluators — professionals who can administer and interpret standardized assessments, conduct situational evaluations, and translate findings into actionable vocational plans — is foundational. Evaluation quality directly affects everything downstream.

Development of genuine supported employment. Moving Korea's supported employment system from a training-first to a placement-first model, with qualified job coaches providing ongoing, individualized workplace support, is the single highest-leverage practice improvement available.

Employer engagement beyond compliance. The most effective vocational rehabilitation programs in the U.S. treat employer relationship-building as a core professional function, not an afterthought. Counselors and job developers who understand labor market needs and can match them with worker capabilities create sustainable placements — not quota-filling arrangements.

Mental health integration. A significant and underserved portion of Korea's disability employment gap involves people with psychiatric disabilities. Evidence from IPS research globally makes clear that integrating employment support with mental health care — not sequencing one before the other — produces substantially better outcomes.

A Final Thought

When I sit across from a client and work through a vocational evaluation together, I am reminded that this work is ultimately about possibility. Not about fitting someone into a category, or filling a slot in a quota, or checking a compliance box. It is about understanding a person's full range of capacities and helping them find where those capacities meet the world's needs.

Korea's decision to raise its disability employment quota is a step worth acknowledging. It represents political will, policy courage, and a recognition that the current numbers are not acceptable. But the number on a quota form will never be the measure of success that matters most. The measure that matters is whether people with disabilities in Korea — and everywhere — are working in jobs that respect their dignity, use their abilities, and allow them to build lives they find meaningful.

That is what vocational rehabilitation, at its best, is for. And that is the work I am committed to.
