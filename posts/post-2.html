An IPS-Informed Approach: What Happened When I Stopped Waiting and Started Walking

Jeongwon (Wonny) Kim, CRC · April 2026 · 6 min read

I moved to Gangneung about eight months ago. Not long after settling in, my mother mentioned my cousin — she is 22, has never worked a day in her life, and has been mostly at home since finishing high school two years ago. Some of that comes from family circumstances that made it harder for her to step out sooner. But two years had passed, and things were not moving.

I decided to intervene. Not formally. Just intentionally — drawing on what I know as a CRC and what I practiced at Aurora Vocational Services in the U.S., where I worked daily within a model called IPS.

What is IPS?

Individual Placement and Support (IPS) is an evidence-based supported employment model originally developed for people with serious mental health conditions. Its core principle: instead of lengthy pre-employment preparation in simulated settings, you go directly into the real world of work — real communities, real workplaces, real relationships — and you build from there. You don't wait until someone is "ready." You help them get ready by being in it. The community itself becomes part of the intervention.

My cousin's situation was not a formal IPS case. But the philosophy fit. She had never worked before — and had been applying to convenience store jobs through an app, alone, without experience, repeatedly rejected before anyone even met her. Two years of that. The approach was not working. It was time to try something different.

Three phases — from the neighborhood to the front door

1
Into the community — weekly walks, no agenda
For about two months, we met once a week in the mornings and walked around the neighborhood — cafes, bakeries, local spots. While we went, I asked casual questions: how many people work here, what does a morning shift look like, could you imagine being in a space like this? The goal was not to talk about jobs. It was to get her physically present in the world of work, repeatedly, until it stopped feeling foreign. IPS calls this community engagement. We called it breakfast.
2
Identifying the right environment — and a goal
One place kept coming up: a small Paris Baguette near her home. One owner, one part-time staff, a manageable space. My cousin said it herself — she could imagine working there. We had also visited enough times that the owner's face was familiar. We set a goal together: before the end of May, visit at least one potential workplace in person. Not apply. Not interview. Just go, see it, talk to someone. That was the whole target.
3
Asking the community — and it said yes
One day I went to the bakery alone and spoke to the owner directly. I explained my cousin's situation: two years at home, no experience, trying to take her first steps. I asked for five minutes — just to show my cousin what the job actually looks like. The owner agreed immediately. At the end she said: "좋은 일 해야죠" — "We should do good things." That, for me, was IPS in a sentence. The community, when you ask it well, will often say yes.

Today

Outside Paris Baguette, Gangneung — before walking in.

This morning, we went together. Before walking in, I gave my cousin three things to do: introduce herself by name, greet the owner properly, and say thank you. If she had questions — hours, what the work involves — she could prepare them in advance and ask.

Inside, the owner barely talked about the job. She talked about being young and figuring things out. She told my cousin that everything in the bakery can be learned, that her staff have stayed for years, that when a spot opens up she will be in touch. She lowered every barrier deliberately. And my cousin — watching that openness — opened up in return. She told the owner she had been rejected everywhere because she had no experience. That she wanted to stop relying on her parents. That she was ready to try.

She wrote down her name and number and handed it over. She said thank you. And we walked out.

Sitting beside her, I noticed that she was a little nervous — eye contact was not easy, and the room was unfamiliar. But what happened was this: the owner shared her own story first. She talked about how she too had once found it hard to step into the world. And my cousin, met with that kind of honesty and warmth, opened up. She said things she had probably not said out loud to a stranger before. The prepared greetings, the introduction, the thank you — she did all of it. Not perfectly, but genuinely.

That, to me, is what the environment can do when you choose it carefully. The right space, the right person, and someone who is actually ready — even if they don't fully know it yet.

On the way out she said: "I think if we do it this way, I can actually find something." And then she talked about being proactive — about not waiting for an app to respond, but walking toward something yourself. That you cannot just sit and hope. That you have to move.

She was proud of herself today. This was the first time she had done something like this — stepped outside the house and the phone screen and into an actual conversation about work and life. The owner gave her courage. And she showed up for it.

That is work readiness: not a checklist, but an attitude learned through experience rather than instruction.

Inside the bakery, after the conversation with the owner. The goal we set together — met before May.

Before today, I had asked my mother to speak to her parents — to let them know what we were doing and make sure they encouraged her to go. I wanted everyone around her to be part of this, not just watching from a distance. When you are trying to help someone take a first step, the people closest to them matter.

After we left the bakery, we sat down inside and had sandwiches together. Then we sent the photo to her parents — and to my mother, who had helped make today possible. A small milestone, shared with the people who were rooting for it.

We hit our goal before May. And today, for the first time, my cousin did something about her future — not on an app, not alone in her room, but in the world, with people, face to face.

That is where it starts.
